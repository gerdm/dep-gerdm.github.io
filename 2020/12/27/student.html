The Student's t-Distribution: A financial approach

Introduction

Picture your favourite stock. Regardless of its average performance, its price has experienced ups and downs that are due to external factors such as pandemics, financial crises, bad publicity, or press leaks. If we only know the price of the stock, we do not know which factor caused which movements in the price of your stock.

Considering the above, how can we model the distribution of returns? One solution would be to assume that both the volatility of the stock and the average rate of return are constant and model the returns as a Normal distribution. The problem with this approach is that big movements in the price of the stock may not be explained by our constant volatility and may be deemed as outliers by our model.

Consider, for example, the daily (linear) returns of TSLA between June 30, 2010 and September 16, 2020. The following graph shows the histogram of historical returns for TSLA, as well as its adjusted normal distribution.

As we can see from the graph above, a Normal distribution is unable to take into account the fluctuation of the stock: less probability is assigned around the mean, and more probability at the tails. Consequently, what we believe to be outlier events may happen more frequently than we would expect and ordinary events less frequently.

To work around this drawback, let us assume that the return of the stock at time ttt is Normally-distributed with constant average rate of return, and a volatility that depends on ttt. Modelling the rate of return in this particular way will leads us to the famous distribution known as the Student’s-t distribution. In effect, we want to model the rate of return not as a single normal distribution, but rather as a collection of Normal distributions.

The data generating process

Let StS_tSt​ be the price of the stock at time ttt; let rtr_trt​ be the arithmetic change of the stock at time ttt, that is rt=St−St−1r_t = S_{t} - S_{t-1}rt​=St​−St−1​. On a given day ttt, an event EtE_tEt​ occurs. EtE_tEt​ has a direct impact on the rate of return of our stock rtr_trt​ by either increasing or decreasing its volatility.1 The greater the volatility, the greater values rtr_trt​ can take on a particular ttt.

More specifically, let us assume that the return for the stock on a given day is normally-distributed with mean μ\muμ and variance σt2\sigma_t^2σt2​ (notice the dependence of σt2\sigma^2_tσt2​ on the time). That is,

rt∼N(μ,σt2) r_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma_t^2) rt​∼N(μ,σt2​)

Since we cannot observe EtE_tEt​ directly (we only know that it exists), we capture the effect that any EtE_tEt​ has over σt2\sigma^2_tσt2​ using a probability distribution. To put it in other words, the return of the stock is driven by two forces: the first force determines the uncertainty at time ttt (the volatility), and the second force, which depends on the first force, determines the actual return at time ttt, namely, rtr_trt​. Statistically, this process can be seen as a procedure with two steps

  1. First, sample σt2\sigma^2_tσt2​ from a given distribution p(σt2)p(\sigma^2_t)p(σt2​); next
  2. sample rtr_trt​ from a distribution N(rt∣μ,σt2)\mathcal{N}(r_t \vert \mu, \sigma^2_t)N(rt​∣μ,σt2​)

The following picture shows this idea. At each time-step ttt, a value σt2\sigma^2_tσt2​ is generated (the orange bars). This generated value is then used to sample from N(μ,σt2)\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2_t)N(μ,σt2​). As we can see, days of high volatility, are correlated with days of higher change in the price StS_tSt​.

Under these assumptions, there not a σ2\sigma_2σ2​ but a whole distribution of them. Alternatively: the volatility is also a random variable: clearly there is no point modelling rtr_trt​ as a normal distribution unless we know σt2\sigma^2_tσt2​ at every ttt.

What’s your expectation?

At this point, we might think that we are in a little bit of a trouble to model rtr_trt​. Thankfully for us, the Head Brewer for the Guiness company, a man named William Sealy Gosset, published a paper under the pseudonym “Student” solving this exact problem (of course, he was interested in measurements of beer and not about your favourite stock).

Instead of modelling rtr_trt​ at every ttt, why don’t we get rid of the term that depends on ttt? If we assume that the probability distribution p(σt2)p(\sigma^2_t)p(σt2​) is the same for all ttt, then we can find a time-independent distribution p(r)p(r)p(r) by averaging-out all possible values of σt2\sigma^2_tσt2​.

More concretely, the average rate of return rrr is distributed according to a probability density function (pdf) given by

p(r)=∫0∞N(r∣μ,σ2)p(σ2)dσ2=Eσ2[N(r∣μ,σ2)] \begin{aligned} p(r) &=\int_0^\infty \mathcal{N}(r\vert\mu, \sigma^2 ) p(\sigma^2) d\sigma^2\\ &=\mathbb{E}_{\sigma^2}[\mathcal{N}(r\vert\mu, \sigma^2 )] \end{aligned} p(r)​=∫0∞​N(r∣μ,σ2)p(σ2)dσ2=Eσ2​[N(r∣μ,σ2)]​
